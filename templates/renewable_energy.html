G20 Renewable energy analysis (1990-2019) and the effect on greenhouse gas emissions

Used datasets:

  • Renewable energy (world) (OECD, 2022)
  • Primary energy supply (world) (OECD, 2022)
  • Total greenhouse gas emissions (Our World in Data, 2022)

What is analysed?

  • Renewable energy consumption of G20 between 1990-2019
  • Total energy consumption of G20 between 1990-2019
  • Change in fraction of renewable energy consumption of G20 between 1990-2019
  • Countries with highest and lowest relative growth of renewable energy consumption against greenhouse gas emissions

What are the assumptions?

  • For convenience Europe (As part of G20) is considered both as EU28 and EU27 (with or without UK)
  • 'Renewable energy' includes: Hydro, geothermal, solar, wind, tide, wave/n and derivatives from solid biofuels, biogasoline, biodiesels, other liquid biofuels, biogases and the renewable fraction of municipal waste (nuclear not included)

What are the units of measurement?

  • Energy Consumption: Mln toe (tonnes of oil equivalent)
  • Greenhouse gas emissions: CO2eq (carbon dioxide gas equivalent)
  • Europe, China and India are big risers. What are the reasons behind this?
  • China's total; energy consumption exploded, with second place going to India
  • Europe and USA seem to have their years of explosive growth behind them
  • Increase in renewable energy consumption of China and India is negligible against increase in total energy consumption
  • The G20 countries with the steepest decrease in renewable energy consumption tend to emit the most greenhouse gasses (relatively)
  • The countries with an increase in renewable energy consumption have emitted less greenhouse gasses over the years

Conclusion and discussion:

A relative increase in renewable energy consumption does indeed lead to less emission of greenhouse gasses.

We are talking of course about G20 countries, so the argument that the diversity of available energy sources in countries at the lower end of the renewable energy consumption ranking are slim does not uphold. These are rich countries with a lot of resources at their disposal. Questions arise about scalability of renewable sources over the last decades. For example, China's explosive rise in demand is apparently not met by a renewable energy industry that is as scalable as the fossil fuel industry. Is it then a question of not enough willpower? Or are the rising demands of the new Chinese middle class no match for the stalling development of renewable energy technologies?

Arguably, Western countries e.g. UK, Europe as a whole, and USA had already undergone the economic boom that characterizes the other members, which puts them in a better position to diversify their energy sources as opposed to upcoming economies, since they do not have to meet demands of a new middle class.

In general it can be said that a relative increase in renewable energy consumption goes hand in hand with a decrease in greenhouse gas emission. The reason for this phenomenon arguably lies in e.g. willpower and a lack of scalability of the renewable energy supply.
